Abstract 
Enterprise adoption of large language models faces cost, latency, and data privacy constraints that 
limit scalable deployment. Small language models (SLMs) with 3 to 8 billion parameters offer a 
viable alternative for specific tasks, but organizations lack  systematic methods to determine which 
tasks are suitable for local SLM deployment versus cloud based large language model (LLM) 
inference. This paper introduces S 3 (Suitability Score for SLM Selection), a multicriteria decision 
making framework that predi cts the appropriate deployment tier before running any inference. S 3 
combines a weighted sum model across six task dimensions with prescreening gate rules that prevent 
compensatory tradeoffs in safety critical scenarios. The framework maps tasks to three deployment 
tiers: Pure SLM, Hybrid, or LLM Only. We validate S 3 across eight enterprise use cases spanning 
cybersecurity, finance, healthcare, legal, and customer service, using seven models from 3 billion 
to 70 billion parameters. All SLMs run locally usin g Ollama on a 16 GB workstation; the LLM 
baseline is served through the Groq API free tier. All eight tier predictions are confirmed by 
benchmark results. Cross framework validation against an independent runtime study (SDDF) yields 
Spearman rank correlation of negative 0.73 (p = 0.01) between expert assigned S 3 scores and data 
driven routing capability. Sensitivity analysis confirms seven of eight assignments are stable across 
five organizational weight profiles. The framework is fully reproducible on consumer hardware. 
Keywords: Small language models; enterprise AI deployment; multicriteria decision making; model 
routing; technology management; weighted sum model; industrial deployment protocol. 
1. Introduction 
The rapid advancement of large language models has created a fundamental tension in enterprise 
technology management. Organizations recognize the transformative potential of generative 
artificial intelligence but face practical constraints: hosted LLM APIs  introduce per query cost,

network latency, and data residency concerns, while locally deployed small language models 
introduce quality and safety risks if applied to tasks that exceed their capability. 1,2,3 Practitioners 
today answer the deployment question informally, through pilot studies, vendor benchmarks, or 
intuition. There is no widely adopted, auditable protocol that turns workload characteristics into a 
defensible deployment decision. 
Small language models, typically ranging from 1 to 10 billion parameters, present a compelling 
alternative for structured enterprise tasks. Recent architectural innovations have demonstrated that 
carefully designed smaller models can match much larger ones  on targeted workloads. 1,2,4 
Distillation, quantisation, and instruction tuning have further narrowed the gap. 5,6,7 However, 
knowing that SLMs can sometimes match LLMs does not tell a technology manager which specific 
task categories are safe for local deployment. 
This paper addresses this gap by introducing S3 (Suitability Score for SLM Selection), a multicriteria 
decision making framework grounded in the weighted sum model. 8,9 S3 evaluates tasks across six 
dimensions: Task Complexity, Output Structure, Stakes, Data Sensitivity, Latency Tolerance, and 
Volume. The framework produces a score between 1 and 5 that maps to one of three deployment 
tiers: Pure SLM, Hybrid, or LLM Only. Two prescreening gate rules encode safety constraints that 
cannot be overridden by the weighted sum, ensuring that life critical or expert level tasks are never 
assigned to an SLM autonomously. 
We validate the framework through a comprehensive empirical study involving eight enterprise use 
cases across six industry domains, benchmarked on seven language models ranging from 3 billion 
to 70 billion parameters. Six SLMs are executed locally using Ollama10 on a single 16 GB MacBook 
Air. The 70 billion parameter LLM baseline is served through the Groq API free tier, 11 which 
provides 14,400 requests per day at no cost. Cross framework validation against an independent 
runtime study (SDDF, developed in p arallel by our research team) yields a Spearman rank 
correlation of negative 0.73 (p = 0.01) between expert assigned S 3 scores and data driven routing 
capability, providing convergent evidence that top down expert assessment and bottom up 
operational telemetry agree. 
The contributions of this paper are as follows. First, we propose a practical multicriteria framework 
that enables technology managers to make SLM deployment decisions before committing resources 
to full scale benchmarking. Second, we demonstrate that expe rt scored dimensions predict 
benchmark outcomes across eight use cases. Third, we establish cross framework convergent 
validity between a top down scoring protocol and a bottom up runtime routing system. Fourth, we 
provide hardware requirements and a deployment protocol suitable for industrial adoption. Fifth, we 
provide a complete reproducibility package including all scripts, gold sets, and configuration files. 
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 reviews related literature. Section 3 
presents the research methodology. Section 4 introduces the S 3 framework. Section 5 describes 
implementation and deployment requirements. Section 6 presents the empirical evaluation design. 
Section 7 reports results and analysis. Section 8 discusses implications for technology managers. 
Section 9 addresses limitations and future research. Section 10 concludes. 
2. Background and Related Work 
2.1 The LLM to SLM Transition 
The transition from large to small language models is motivated by three factors: cost, latency, and 
data governance. Brown and colleagues 12 demonstrated that language models at the 175 billion

parameter scale achieve remarkable few shot performance, but at prohibitive inference costs for 
high volume enterprise tasks. Scaling laws established by Kaplan and colleagues 13 and refined by 
Hoffmann and colleagues 14 showed that compute optimal training produces capable models at 
smaller scales. Touvron and colleagues 1 released LLaMA, demonstrating competitive performance 
with open weights. Jiang and colleagues2 showed that Mistral 7B matches or exceeds larger models 
through architectural innovations including grouped query attention and sliding window attention. 
Abdin and colleagues 15 demonstrated that the Phi series achieves strong performance at the 3 to 4 
billion parameter scale. 
Subramanian, Elango, and Gungor 16 demonstrated that SLMs under 4 billion parameters achieve 
competitive performance on targeted NLP tasks when appropriately prompted. Lu and colleagues 17 
provided a systematic evaluation of SLMs for edge deployment. Fan and colleagues 18 explored 
collaborative frameworks where small and large models work together for cost effectiveness. These 
findings establish that SLMs are viable for specific enterprise tasks but do not address the prior 
question of which tasks are suitable for SLM deployment. 
2.2 Model Routing and Cascading Frameworks 
Several frameworks address the model selection problem at runtime. Ding and colleagues proposed 
Hybrid LLM,19 a cost efficient query routing system that directs queries to appropriate model tiers 
based on predicted difficulty. Ong and colleagues introduced RouteLLM, 20 which learns routing 
policies from preference data. Hu and colleagues published RouterBench, 21 a benchmark for 
evaluating multi LLM routing systems. Aggarwal and colleagues proposed AutoMix,22 an automatic 
model mixing approach. Dekoninck and colleagues presented a unified approach to routing and 
cascading.23 
These routing frameworks operate at the query level during runtime. They do not address the prior 
question that technology managers face: should we deploy an SLM for this task category at all? S 3 
addresses this strategic question before any production traffic exists. 
2.3 Multicriteria Decision Making in Technology Management 
Multicriteria decision making methods have a long history in technology management. The Analytic 
Hierarchy Process introduced by Saaty 24 enables structured comparison through pairwise criteria 
weighting. Hwang and Yoon25 developed TOPSIS as a distance based ranking method. Edwards and 
Barron26 proposed SMARTS for simplified multiattribute utility measurement. Brans and Vincke 27 
introduced PROMETHEE as a preference ranking method. Keeney and Raiffa 28 formalised 
multiattribute utility theory with rigorous preference elicitation. 
The weighted sum model is the canonical MCDM method for decisions with numeric, 
commensurable criteria. 8 Triantaphyllou9 provided a comparative study of MCDM methods. 
Triantaphyllou and Sanchez 29 provided specific procedures for determining the minimum weight 
change needed to alter a decision. Zavadskas and Turskis 30 surveyed MCDM applications in 
economics. Zopounidis and Doumpos31 applied multicriteria methods to financial sorting decisions. 
Belahcene and colleagues32 surveyed multiple criteria sorting models. We adopt WSM because our 
six criteria are naturally expressed on a common 1 to 5 scale and because the simpler formula 
supports stakeholder communication. 
2.4 Technology Adoption Frameworks 
Enterprise AI deployment decisions occur within technology adoption frameworks. The Technology 
Acceptance Model 33 and its extension UTAUT 34 provide theoretical foundations for understanding

adoption barriers. Tornatzky and Fleischer 35 examined how organizational context shapes 
technology adoption processes. Rogers 36 described how innovations diffuse through organizations. 
S3 provides the structured evaluation that organizations need to move beyond pilot stages into 
production deployment. 
2.5 AI Governance and Risk Frameworks 
The governance dimension is addressed through the NIST AI Risk Management Framework 37 and 
the European Union AI Act, 38 which classify AI systems by risk level and mandate proportional 
safeguards. Floridi and colleagues 39 proposed an ethical framework for AI deployment. Our gate 
rules (Section 4.4) operationalize these risk based requirements: stakes equal to five triggers 
mandatory human oversight regardless of the model's quantitative performance. 
2.6 The Critical Gap 
The literature presents a gap between strategic deployment guidance and operational routing. 
Existing MCDM frameworks have not been applied to the specific problem of SLM tier assignment. 
Existing routing frameworks assume the deployment decision has already been made. No published 
work provides an auditable, pre deployment scoring protocol grounded in MCDM theory that is 
validated both empirically and against an independent runtime system. This paper fills that gap. 
3. Research Methodology 
3.1 Design Science Research Paradigm 
We follow the design science research paradigm, 40,41 which positions the artefact, in our case the S 3 
scoring protocol, as the research contribution. Design science requires demonstration and evaluation 
against relevance and rigour criteria. Relevance is established by the identified research gap: 
enterprises lack a systematic method for SLM deployment decisions. Rigour is established through 
grounding in MCDM theory, 8,9 structured evaluation across eight use cases, and cross framework 
validation. 
3.2 Framework Development 
The S3 framework was developed through five steps. First, we identified the decision attributes that 
enterprise practitioners evaluate when considering SLM deployment, drawing on published 
deployment case studies and the risk management literature. 37,38 Second, we operationalized these 
attributes as six scored dimensions with anchored 1 to 5 scales (Table 1). Third, we selected the 
WSM8 as the aggregation method due to its transparency and interpretability in management 
settings. Fourth, we identified non compensatory safety constraints and encoded them as gate rules 
following the multiattribute utility theory tradition.28 Fifth, we calibrated tier thresholds against a set 
of eight pilot use cases. 
3.3 Expert Elicitation Protocol 
All eight use cases were scored by the first author, reviewed by a second domain expert, and 
reconciled through discussion. Each dimension has published 1 to 5 anchors (Table 1) that constrain 
interpretation. This approach is structurally identical to clin ical severity scoring systems such as 
APACHE II42 and CURB 65, 43 both of which rely on anchored expert assessment and are accepted 
for clinical decisions affecting patient outcomes. The scoring worksheet, including per dimension 
rationale for each use case, was committed to the project repository before any model inference was

3.4 Research Questions 
We investigate four research questions: 
• RQ1 (Tier Prediction): Does the S³ score correctly predict whether an SLM can serve a 
given enterprise workload? 
• RQ2 (Gate Rule Necessity): Do non compensatory gate rules prevent unsafe deployments 
that the weighted sum alone would miss? 
• RQ3 (Cross Framework Convergence): Do expert assigned S³ scores correlate with data 
driven runtime routing capability from an independent study? 
• RQ4 (Robustness): Are tier assignments stable under reasonable perturbations of the weight 
vector?

4.2 The S³ Formula 
The suitability score is the weighted sum model8,9 normalized to the 1 to 5 scale: 
S³ = ( Σ scoreᵢ · wᵢ ) / ( Σ 5 · wᵢ ) × 5 
where scorei is in {1, 2, 3, 4, 5} and wi is the dimension weight. This formula is the textbook WSM 
aggregation.9 A lower S3 indicates higher suitability for SLM deployment; a higher S 3 indicates the 
workload demands LLM capability. 
4.3 Tier Mapping 

===== PAGE 6 =====

Scores map to deployment tiers using two calibrated thresholds: 
• Pure SLM: S³ ≤ 3.2. The SLM can serve this workload autonomously. 
• Hybrid: 3.2 < S³ ≤ 4.0. The SLM serves a subset of queries; the remainder escalate to the 
LLM baseline. 
• LLM Only: S³ > 4.0. The workload requires the LLM, with human oversight for safety 
critical cases. 
The thresholds were calibrated on the eight pilot use cases so that tier boundaries align with 
empirical capability transitions. Sensitivity to this calibration is reported in Section 7.8. 
4.4 Prescreening Gate Rules 
Weighted sums compensate low scores on one criterion with high scores on another. In safety 
sensitive settings, this behavior is undesirable. We therefore add three non compensatory gate rules 
following the multiattribute utility theory tradition:28 
• Hard Rule 1: SK = 5 → Disqualified. Route to LLM Only regardless of formula score. 
• Hard Rule 2: TC = 5 AND SK ≥ 4 → LLM Only regardless of formula score. 
• Flag Rule: SK ≥ 4 → Minimum tier is Hybrid. The formula may push higher but not lower. 
Gate rules are evaluated before the formula. A disqualified use case never enters the scoring 
arithmetic for tier assignment, although the formula value is recorded for audit purposes. The 
necessity of these rules is demonstrated empirically in Section 7.2  and through reverse calculation 
in Appendix D. 
4.5 SDDF as Runtime Implementation 
S3 produces the deployment blueprint. The SLM Deployment Decision Framework (SDDF), 
developed in parallel by our research team, is the runtime implementation that tests whether the 
blueprint holds in practice. SDDF logs pass/fail outcomes from live inference , computes per task 
capability and risk bounds using Wilson confidence intervals, 44 and routes individual queries to 
either the SLM or the LLM baseline. The runtime policy enforcement is summarized in Table 2. 
SDDF is treated in this paper as independent  validation evidence; the full SDDF methodology is 
reported separately.

5. Implementation and Deployment 
5.1 Model Optimization Strategies 
Three optimization strategies enable SLM deployment on commodity hardware. Post training 
quantization reduces model precision to lower memory footprint and increase throughput. GPTQ 45 
achieves significant throughput improvement through one shot second order weight quantization. 
AWQ46 identifies salient weight channels from activation distributions, achieving lower perplexity 

===== PAGE 7 =====

Decision Framework for SLMs in Enterprise AI 
7 
degradation than uniform quantization. All SLMs in our study use Q4_K_M quantization through 
Ollama, which applies 4 bit mixed precision quantization suitable for consumer hardware. 
Knowledge distillation 5 transfers capability from larger teacher models to smaller student models 
during training. Low rank adaptation (LoRA) 47 enables parameter efficient fine tuning by training 
low rank decomposition matrices rather than full weight updates. QLoRA 6 combines quantization 
with LoRA for memory efficient fine tuning. These techniques are not applied in our study (we use 
pretrained models as released) but are relevant for organizations seeking to specialize SLMs for 
specific enterprise tasks.

For each use case we constructed a 100 item gold set partitioned into 70 development and 30 test

before benchmarking. All system prompts use temperature 0 and three inference seeds (42, 43, 44)

6.4 Metrics 
For classification tasks we report accuracy and SLM versus LLM parity (SLM accuracy divided by 
LLM accuracy) with 95% Wilson confidence intervals.44 For structured extraction (UC2) we report 
JSON validity (fraction of responses that parse against the declared schema). For the generative task 
(UC8) we report an LLM as judge score 49 using the baseline LLM as judge with a fixed rubric and 
temperature 0. Latency is reported at P50, P95, and P99. Paired comparisons use a two sided paired

7.3 Pure SLM Tier Results 
UC4 (product review sentiment) is the strongest Pure SLM case: Mistral 7B at 95.5% accuracy with 
98.8% parity to the LLM baseline (96.7%). A paired t test yields t = 0.89, p = 0.38, indicating no

gap between the best SLM and the LLM baseline is not statistically distinguishable. 
7.4 Hybrid Tier Results 
UC5 (automated code review) is the most challenging Hybrid case: the top SLM (Llama 3.1 8B) 
achieves only 46.7% accuracy versus the LLM's 61.1% (76.4% parity). This confirms that the SLM 
alone is insufficient and that an escalation mechanism to the LLM bas eline is required. UC7 (legal 
contract risk) shows 53.3% versus 57.8% (92.2% parity), closer to the Pure SLM boundary but 
below the 95% threshold. UC1 (SMS threat detection) achieves parity of 100% but is assigned 
Hybrid by the Flag Rule because SK = 4 mandates human oversight for a task where missed threats 
cause direct harm. 
7.5 Latency 
On the 16 GB workstation, end to end SLM latency at P95 stays under 500 ms for the confirmed

interactive SLA. The LLM baseline through Groq has a median latency of appr oximately 700 ms 
from Dallas, dominated by network round trip. All Pure SLM use cases meet the interactive SLA 
class defined in Section 5.4. 
7.6 Three Layer Validation

• Layer 1 (top down): Expert S³ scores predict tiers; benchmarks test whether predictions 
hold. 
• Layer 2 (bottom up): Empirical results infer the most defensible tier; comparison with S³ 
prediction reveals agreement or disagreement. 
• Layer 3 (reverse consistency): For each empirical tier, determine whether a dimension 
profile compatible with that tier can be reconstructed.

7.7 Cross Framework Validation (RQ3) 
To test convergent validity, we compute the Spearman rank correlation 51 between S 3 scores and 
SDDF routing capability on an independent set of eight NLP task families (classification, code 
generation, information extraction, instruction following, mathematics, retrieval grounded QA, 
summarization, and text generation). SDDF computes per  task routing capability from operational 
telemetry collected across three SLMs (Qwen 2.5 at 0.5B, 3B, and 7B) and one LLM baseline 
(Llama 3.3 70B via Groq), with 120 samples per model per task family. 
The observed correlation is rho = negative 0.73 with p = 0.01, supporting RQ3. Tasks that S 3 rates 
as harder for SLMs (higher score) show lower routing capability in SDDF (fewer queries 
successfully handled locally). Tier level agreement is 6 of 8 (75%). The two disagreements are cases 
where SDDF's learned capability exceeds what S3's gate rules permit; these are resolved in favor of 
S3 by policy, since the blueprint's safety constraints take precedence over runtime optimism. Full 
results are in Appendix F.

can remove a binary gate condition. 
8. Discussion and Managerial Implications 
8.1 Key Findings 
Four findings emerge from this study. First, the S 3 framework correctly predicts deployment tiers 
for all eight enterprise use cases, with Pure SLM cases achieving parity at or above 95% and gate 
locked cases exhibiting the specific failure modes that gate rules target. Second, gate rules are 
necessary safety mechanisms, not conservative overhead: three use cases (UC6, UC7, UC8) would 
be misclassified or inadequately safeguarded without them (see Appendix D). Third, the Spearman

rank correlation of negative 0.73 (p = 0.01) between expert assigned scores and independent runtime 
routing capability establishes convergent validity across two entirely different assessment 
methodologies. Fourth, tier assignments are robust: seven of eig ht are stable across five weight 
profiles, and the sole sensitive case is correctly flagged. 
8.2 Gate Rules as Safety Mechanisms 
UC6 demonstrates why aggregate accuracy is an insufficient safety metric. The top SLM achieves 
106.4% parity by overall accuracy, yet systematically fails on the URGENT triage class. In a clinical 
setting, this class collapse would result in delayed treatm ent for patients requiring immediate care. 
Hard Rule 1 (SK = 5) catches this failure mode categorically, without relying on any threshold 
calibration. UC8 demonstrates the complementary case: a formula value that would assign Pure 
SLM is overridden by Hard Rule 2 because expert level generative tasks under significant financial 
stakes should not be served autonomously by an SLM. The reverse calculations in Appendix D 
quantify exactly what would go wrong without these safeguards. 
8.3 Hardware and Cost Implications 
For the three confirmed Pure SLM use cases (UC2, UC3, UC4), organizations can serve all queries 
locally on commodity hardware at zero marginal inference cost. The minimum requirement is an 8 
GB business workstation for 3B to 4B models, or a 16 GB workstation for 7B to 8B models (Table 
4). No GPU is required. For Hybrid use cases, the escalation rate determines cost savings: UC1 
routes the majority of queries locally, escalating only high uncertainty cases to the LLM API. For 
high volume tasks at tens of tho usands of queries per day, the cost differential between local SLM 
inference (zero marginal cost) and API calls is substantial. 
8.4 Practical Guidelines for Technology Managers 
Based on our results, we recommend the following deployment protocol: 
• Step 1: Score the workload on six dimensions using Table 1 anchors. Include a domain 
expert and a compliance reviewer in the scoring session. 
• Step 2: Check gate rules first. If SK = 5 or (TC = 5 and SK >= 4), assign LLM Only with 
human oversight. No further analysis is needed. 
• Step 3: Compute S³ and assign the tier. If the score is within 0.2 of a tier boundary, flag it as 
sensitive and request additional pilot data. 
• Step 4: Run a focused benchmark on 30 test items with the top two candidate SLMs. 
Compare against the LLM baseline using parity at the 95% threshold. 
• Step 5: Deploy runtime routing once 2 to 4 weeks of production telemetry is available. 
Monitor per task routing capability and update weekly. 
• Step 6: Re score S³ when the workload's dimensions materially change (new data type, new 
SLA, new regulatory scope). 
Three stage output filtering should be applied in production: input sanitization, constrained decoding 
to prevent malformed outputs, and post generation semantic checks before execution. For tasks with 
SK >= 4, all SLM outputs should pass through a human review gate before reaching end users. 
9. Limitations and Future Research 
9.1 Threats to Validity 

===== PAGE 13 =====

Decision Framework for SLMs in Enterprise AI 
13 
Construct validity. The six S3 dimensions were selected by the research team. Other dimension sets 
are defensible; the cross framework agreement with SDDF provides evidence but not proof that our 
six are sufficient. 
Internal validity. Expert scoring is subjective. We mitigated this through dual scoring and 
reconciliation, anchored scales identical in structure to clinical severity instruments, 42,43 and 
commitment of scores before benchmarking. Rater bias cannot be fully eliminated with two raters. 
External validity. Eight use cases from one academic enterprise context do not generalize to all 
domains. The framework is intended to be re scored per workload, not transferred wholesale. 
Statistical validity. The Spearman correlation uses n = 8 task families, which is small. 52 We report 
the exact p value and note that larger scale replication is needed. 
9.2 Automated Scoring as Future Work 
A concurrent effort explored automated S 3 scoring from task prompts, using keyword heuristics, 
regular expressions, and token count features. We did not adopt automated scoring in this paper 
because three dimensions, SK (Stakes), DS (Data Sensitivity), and parts of LT (Latency Tolerance), 
depend on enterprise context that is not recoverable from the task prompt alone. Gate rules are binary 
safety constraints; a single misclassification of SK can invert the deployment decision. UC6 
demonstrates this: without  contextual knowledge that the task involves emergency department 
triage, an automated scorer would need to infer SK = 5 from prompt keywords alone, a fragile and 
safety critical dependency. We reserve automated scoring for future work, where we plan to 
combine prompt features with a structured intake form and a retrieval component that surfaces 
similar prior workloads. 
9.3 Other Future Directions 
• Larger cross framework study (n >= 30 task families) to enable parametric inference on the 
Spearman estimate. 
• Dynamic operational zones at runtime offering conservative, balanced, and aggressive 
routing strategies. 
• Continuous validation with change point detection for streaming telemetry. 
• Application to multimodal SLMs as vision language models reach the 3B to 8B parameter 
range. 
• Inter rater reliability study with n >= 5 independent scorers per use case. 
• Integration of information theoretic difficulty measures from Shannon entropy³⁶ into the 
scoring dimensions. 
10. Conclusion 
We presented S 3, a six dimension weighted sum decision framework for assigning enterprise AI 
workloads to Pure SLM, Hybrid, or LLM Only deployment tiers. The framework combines a WSM 
formula grounded in MCDM theory with prescreening gate rules that enforce non compensato ry 
safety constraints for high stakes and expert level tasks. 
Four findings support the framework's validity. First, all eight tier predictions are empirically 
confirmed, with Pure SLM cases achieving parity at or above 95% and gate locked cases exhibiting 
the specific failure modes that gate rules target. Second, ga te rules are necessary: three use cases 
would be misclassified or inadequately safeguarded without them. Third, cross framework

Appendix A. Worked S³ Calculation: UC4 (Product Review Sentiment) 
UC4 is a 3 class sentiment classification task (POSITIVE, NEGATIVE, NEUTRAL) on publicly 
available product reviews. 
Dimension scores: TC = 1 (near binary difficulty), OS = 5 (single word from closed vocabulary), 
SK = 1 (no person harmed), DS = 1 (public data), LT = 3 (interactive, P95 < 2 seconds), VL = 3 
(tens of thousands per day). 
Weighted sum computation: 
1 × 3 + 5 × 2 + 1 × 4 + 1 × 2 + 3 × 3 + 3 × 1 = 3 + 10 + 4 + 2 + 9 + 3 = 31 
S³ = (31 / 75) × 5 = 2.07 
Gate check: SK = 1 → Pass. No gate rule applies. 
Tier assignment: 2.07 ≤ 3.2 → Pure SLM. 
Empirical confirmation: Mistral 7B achieves 95.5% accuracy on the 30 item test split (98.8% 
parity to LLM baseline at 96.7%). Three additional SLMs achieve parity above 95%. Paired t test: 
t = 0.89, p = 0.38 (not significant). No class level collapse observed. Assignment confirme d at all 
three validation layers. 
Appendix B. Gate Rule Override: UC6 (Clinical Triage, Hard Rule 1) 
UC6 is a 4 class triage priority classification (CRITICAL, URGENT, SEMIURGENT, 
NONURGENT) for emergency department patients. 
Dimension scores: TC = 4 (multi step clinical reasoning), OS = 5 (single label), SK = 5 (patient 
death from under triage), DS = 4 (PHI under HIPAA), LT = 4 (near real time), VL = 2 (hundreds 
per day). 
Weighted sum: 4 × 3 + 5 × 2 + 5 × 4 + 4 × 2 + 4 × 3 + 2 × 1 = 12 + 10 + 20 + 8 + 12 + 2 = 64. S³ = 
(64/75) × 5 = 4.27. 
Gate: SK = 5 triggers Hard Rule 1 → Disqualified → LLM Only with mandatory human oversight. 
Why the gate is load bearing: The top SLM (Llama 3.1 8B) achieves 73.3% overall accuracy, 
which is 106.4% parity to the LLM baseline (68.9%). By aggregate metrics, the SLM appears 
competitive. However, class level analysis shows SLM recall on the URGENT class at 0% to 64% 
across models, versus 82% for the LLM. Systematic under triage of emergent cases is exactly the 
failure mode Hard Rule 1 targets. The formula score (4.27 > 4.0) already implies LLM Only; the 
gate provides a categorical guarantee robust to any weight perturbation.
