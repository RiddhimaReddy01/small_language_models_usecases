A UNIFIED FRAMEWORK FOR ENTERPRISE SLM DEPLOYMENT: S³ POLICY AND SDDF EMPIRICAL VALIDATION
Smitha Rajappa, Riddhima Ramasahayam Reddy, Rohit Savant, Yashraj Saxena, Smita Sengupta, Ashim Bose
Naveen Jindal School of Management, University of Texas at Dallas, Richardson, TX 75080, USA
Abstract
Enterprise adoption of generative artificial intelligence faces a structural constraint that conventional deployment practice does not address: organizations lack an auditable, pre-deployment protocol for deciding which workloads are suitable for locally served small language models (SLMs) and which require cloud-hosted large language model (LLM) inference. This paper introduces two complementary frameworks developed jointly to resolve this constraint. The S³ framework (Suitability Score for SLM Selection) is a multicriteria decision-making instrument that evaluates an enterprise workload on six dimensions—Task Complexity, Output Structure, Stakes, Data Sensitivity, Latency Tolerance, and Volume—using a weighted sum model with prescreening gate rules that enforce non-compensatory safety constraints. The SLM Deployment Decision Framework (SDDF, version 3) is an empirical validation layer operating at the NLP task-family level through a three-phase train-validate-test protocol that estimates per-model capability curves C₂(d) and risk surfaces R₂(d) across difficulty levels, selects an optimal routing threshold τ*, and evaluates frozen artifacts on held-out inputs. We validate the unified framework across eight enterprise use cases spanning cybersecurity, finance, healthcare, legal, and customer service domains. S³ scores are benchmarked against seven language models ranging from 3 billion to 70 billion parameters; all eight tier predictions are confirmed by benchmark outcomes. Cross-framework validation against SDDF yields a Spearman rank correlation of −0.726 (p = 0.010) between expert-assigned S³ scores and data-driven routing capability across eight NLP task families, establishing convergent validity between top-down expert assessment and bottom-up operational telemetry. The complete experiment is reproducible on a 16 GB Apple M2 Pro (macOS) or a 16 GB AMD Ryzen 7 8840HS workstation (Windows), without requiring a discrete GPU.
Keywords: small language models; enterprise AI deployment; multicriteria decision making; model routing; technology management; weighted sum model; empirical validation; difficulty-based capability estimation.
1. Introduction
Enterprise adoption of generative artificial intelligence has created a structural decision problem for technology managers. Hosted large language model application programming interfaces offer broad capability but impose three constraints that limit scalable deployment at volume: per-query inference costs that compound across high-throughput workloads, network round-trip latency that violates interactive service-level agreements, and data residency requirements that conflict with healthcare, financial, and legal compliance frameworks such as HIPAA and GDPR. Organizations processing conversational workloads through hosted LLM APIs face monthly infrastructure costs that can reach $50,000 to $100,000 for modest volumes, while locally served small language models under 10 billion parameters execute equivalent structured tasks at $127 to $500 per month—a cost differential of one to two orders of magnitude driven by the 10- to 30-fold reduction in compute and energy demand when serving a 7-billion-parameter local model instead of a 70- to 175-billion-parameter hosted model. Architectural advances including grouped query attention, sliding window attention, and mixed-precision quantization have significantly narrowed the quality gap between small and large models on domain-specific tasks, and regulatory momentum toward on-premise processing is accelerating SLM adoption across industries handling sensitive data.
Small language models present a viable alternative for structured enterprise workloads. Fine-tuned and instruction-tuned SLMs have been shown to match or exceed hosted LLMs on targeted classification, information extraction, and summarization tasks at substantially lower operational cost. However, the existence of capable SLMs does not resolve the prior strategic question that technology managers face before any deployment is committed: which specific enterprise tasks are suitable for local SLM deployment, and which require the broader capability, oversight mechanisms, or safety guarantees of a hosted LLM? This question is answered today through pilot studies, vendor benchmarks, or managerial intuition—none of which produces an auditable, reproducible, pre-deployment decision protocol.
The literature reveals a gap between two bodies of work that address adjacent but distinct problems. Runtime model routing frameworks—including Hybrid LLM, RouteLLM, AutoMix, and the unified cascading approach of Dekoninck and colleagues—optimize model selection at the query level during inference. They presuppose that the deployment architecture has already been decided and focus on per-instance cost-quality tradeoffs. Multicriteria decision-making methods from the technology management literature provide structured frameworks for pre-deployment evaluation decisions but have not been applied to the specific problem of SLM tier assignment. No published framework provides an auditable, pre-deployment scoring protocol that maps workload characteristics to a deployment tier and is validated both empirically and against an independent runtime system.
This paper addresses that gap through two complementary frameworks developed jointly by our research team. The S³ framework (Suitability Score for SLM Selection) is a policy-layer instrument applied at the enterprise use-case level before any inference is executed. A domain expert evaluates the target workload on six dimensions—Task Complexity (TC), Output Structure (OS), Stakes (SK), Data Sensitivity (DS), Latency Tolerance (LT), and Volume (VL)—using anchored integer scales from 1 to 5. A weighted sum model normalized to the same scale produces an S³ score, with the weight vector w = (3, 2, 4, 2, 3, 1) placing the heaviest emphasis on stakes, followed by task complexity and latency. Three prescreening gate rules encode non-compensatory safety constraints: Hard Rule 1 disqualifies any workload where SK = 5, regardless of formula score; Hard Rule 2 disqualifies workloads where TC = 5 and SK ≥0 4; and the Flag Rule enforces a minimum tier of Hybrid for any workload where SK ≥0 4. Gate rules are evaluated before the formula and cannot be overridden by any weight perturbation. The score maps to one of three deployment tiers—Pure SLM, Hybrid, or LLM Only—through two calibrated thresholds.
The SLM Deployment Decision Framework (SDDF, version 3) is an empirical validation layer that operates at the NLP task-family level. SDDF follows a three-phase protocol. In the train phase, a logistic regression difficulty model is fit on per-family difficulty features extracted from inference inputs, with binary failure signals derived from benchmark outcomes. The feature extractor (sddf/difficulty.py) computes 20 features per input, including task-specific signals such as classification ambiguity, negation density, domain shift, mathematical numeric and symbol density, and instruction format strictness, combined with general complexity proxies including token count, entropy, and reasoning-constraint interaction terms. In the validation phase, capability curves C₂(d) and risk surfaces R₂(d) are estimated per model per task family across difficulty levels, and an optimal routing threshold τ* is selected as the maximum difficulty at which the model satisfies both a minimum capability constraint (τ₂ap = 0.65) and a maximum risk constraint (τrisk = 0.30). In the test phase, frozen artifacts are evaluated on held-out inputs, producing per-instance predictions, routing decisions, and calibration metrics including ROC-AUC, PR-AUC, Brier score, and expected calibration error. At runtime, individual query difficulty is scored against the stored τ* to route the query to either the local SLM or the LLM baseline. The SDDF codebase, implemented in Python across the sddf/ module and the framework/benchmarking/ pipeline, uses Qwen 2.5 at 0.5B, 3B, and 7B parameters as SLM candidates, with Llama 3.3-70B Versatile served through the Groq API as the LLM baseline.
The contributions of this paper are as follows. First, we introduce S³, a practical MCDM framework enabling technology managers to assign enterprise AI workloads to deployment tiers before any inference infrastructure is provisioned. Second, we validate S³ across eight enterprise use cases spanning cybersecurity, finance, healthcare, legal, and customer service domains, benchmarked against seven language models ranging from 3B to 70B parameters. All eight tier predictions are confirmed by benchmark outcomes: three Pure SLM use cases achieve SLM parity at or above 95%, three Hybrid cases confirm that escalation mechanisms are necessary, and two LLM Only gate-locked cases exhibit the specific failure modes—clinical under-triage and financial hallucination—that the gate rules are designed to prevent. Third, we present SDDF v3 as a fully specified three-phase empirical validation protocol, including its difficulty feature extractor, logistic regression failure model, capability and risk estimation curves, and threshold calibration procedure. Fourth, we establish cross-framework convergent validity: the Spearman rank correlation between expert-assigned S³ scores and SDDF-measured routing capability across eight NLP task families is −0.726 (p = 0.010), confirming that top-down expert assessment and bottom-up operational telemetry measure the same underlying construct. Fifth, we provide a complete reproducibility package: all scripts, gold evaluation sets, raw inference outputs, evaluation reports, and scoring worksheets are publicly available in two companion repositories. The full benchmark is executable on a 16 GB Apple M2 Pro (macOS, Ollama-served SLMs) or a 16 GB AMD Ryzen 7 8840HS workstation (Windows, Groq-served baseline), without requiring a discrete GPU.
